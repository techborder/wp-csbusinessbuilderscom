Burn the Boats

Posted on January 26th, by Tara Patty in Business Leadership. Comments Off

by Chris Schafer

SOLID Operators, LLC

Legend has it that Alexander the Great, after burning his boats, turned to his men and said, We go home in Persian boats or we die! Entrepreneurs, business owners, and executives that are thrust into difficult business situations have a similar decision to make, burn the boats or get back in and sail home.

Adapting to the difficult business situations

requires courage, honest analysis, intelligence, and a mindset focused on overcoming obstacles and setbacks.

Alexander’s decision to destroy his only way out hardened his resolve to find the winning formula. That much is a given, but being a business owner takes more than self-sacrifice and commitment to a given cause. Most business leaders understand sacrifice and going all in. A local micro brewery owner I know has not paid himself in more than eight years and business is booming.

The question that beckons to be answered is, what is it that possesses the entrepreneur to give up everything and burn the proverbial boats for a business objective? More importantly what makes some succeed where so many fail? Possible answers to this question include: they are all nuts and require psychiatric help, business owners know something that the rest of us don’t, and the best one of all time has to be, they are born with it.

Alexander the Great and those throughout history that are considered leaders with unparalleled abilities come from many backgrounds and educations. This is also true for successful entrepreneurs, Henry Ford and Steve Jobs, writers Willian Shakespeare and world leaders Winston Churchill. This points to the simple fact that neither education nor background are formulas for being successful in any endeavor.

Debates rage about successful leaders being endowed with the ability to lead at birth or if they are made through schools and experience. Most involved in this debate would agree that it’s a mixture of both. After serving under some of the best military leader’s in our nations elite forces, I am of the belief that it is more about mindset than degrees and pedigrees.

Mindset is rarely mentioned in business publications or academia as criteria for success. The reason behind this is quite simple: like leadership, mindset is difficult to teach and therefore it must be inherent to the individual. However mindset is easily defined as defeatist or optimist and is readily observed in attitudes. I submit the following example.

My youngest is seven, and like most seven year olds is defeated at math problems that have three or more digits. Currently math appears to be a worthless endeavor for her; however the other day she wanted a soda for lunch. This came with a problem set of getting into the cardboard case. I informed her that if she wanted a soda for lunch, she would have to get it out for herself.

“No problem, Daddy, I know how to get one out,” she told me as I left to attend to other lunch-making duties. Once I returned, I noticed that a portion of the soda box was in the kitchen sink and the counter was wet.

“What happened, Sam?” I asked looking at the soaked cardboard in the sink. She proceeded to tell me that she used a pair of scissors to jab at the top of the box, puncturing a hole in one of the cans causing it to explode. Her mindset was adaptive, optimistic, and focused in obtaining the goal. Yes, her success cost one soda and cleaning the kitchen, but more importantly the lesson she learned will last a lifetime.

Mindset is established by our experiences, environment, attitudes and culture. It can be learned and fostered —Alexander was tutored by Aristotle. Facing odds and finding suitable solutions takes focusing on the goal and having the courage to close the door to the exit.

Alexander the Great is considered one of histories most successful commanders, not because he was smarter or “born with it,” – his success lied within his mindset.
